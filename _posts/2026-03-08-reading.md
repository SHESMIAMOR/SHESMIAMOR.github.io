# Original Passage

## Paragraph A

Whether we are aware of it or not, all around us, “persuaders” are at work. We have become used to a world where powerful images, catchy soundbites and pushy advertising bombard us every day. Supermarkets, chain stores, politicians, advertisers and salespeople know exactly how to manipulate us with their images, ideas or produce. When it comes to persuading us, they employ skilled experts to use the best psychological tricks, meaning even the cautious among us may be influenced without realizing it.

## Paragraph B

Supermarkets have always led the way in this type of persuasion. We now buy 75% of our food from supermarket chains, and most of us leave having purchased products we didn't intend to buy.

In one early stroke of genius, Sainsbury introduced shopping baskets to its stores in the 1950s. This meant that shoppers would now browse and pick up items that they formerly would have ignored.

Soon after came shopping trolleys, and the novelty attracted more shoppers.

Professor Merlin Stone, IBM Professor of Relationship Marketing at Bristol Business School, says aisles are deliberately laid out to maximize profits.

Stores are designed around our **money-rich, time-poor lives**, with high-turnover products commanding positions at the front, whereas low-turnover products such as clothes and electrical goods are stocked at the back of the store.

## Paragraph C

Ralph Stone, an associate professor at the University of Chicago, believes that stores use **“stalling tactics”** to keep us in the store longer, because the more time we spend, the more we will buy.

They make great efforts to make the store environment pleasant, even playing music to induce a relaxed state of mind.

Some stores take advantage of the air from the in-store bakery to tempt shoppers with the aroma.

In the USA, fake aromas are sometimes used because studies have shown that smell is the most evocative and subliminal of our senses and even prompts us to spend more.

In one experiment, a casino that perfumed half of its building saw profits rise significantly in the scent-filled areas.

Another success story from the supermarkets' perspective is the **loyalty card**.

Shoppers may feel that they are being rewarded for their loyalty, but they are also sharing information about their shopping habits.

## Paragraph D

It doesn't matter how wise we think we are to their tactics—the advertising industry still manages to influence us.

Researchers at the University of Florida concluded that **emotional responses dominate over cognition** when we react to adverts.

Researcher Laurette Dube explains that when attitudes are based on logical reasoning, advertisers use **informative appeals**.

This is particularly effective for functional products such as bleach.

When attitudes are based on emotions, advertisements aim to tap into feelings.

## Paragraph E

Advertisers play on a wide range of emotions.

Some advertisements aim to be **aspirational**, appealing to our self-esteem.

Others focus on our need to feel **safe**, such as insurance advertisements.

Others create a desire to **belong to a group**, such as fashion advertising.

Celebrities are often used as a shortcut because they are seen as **successful** or **familiar**.

One survey of 4000 advertising campaigns found that advertisements with celebrities were **10% more effective**.

Humour is also effective because it stimulates a rapid emotional response.

Research shows amusing advertisements are remembered longer than serious ones.

## Paragraph F

Professional salespeople are highly skilled and can sell a wide variety of products.

They apply psychological techniques that increase a shopper's perception of how much they need a product.

Shoppers usually have **preconceived requirements** when evaluating a product.

Salespeople attempt to discover these requirements and explain the product in ways that appeal to individual shoppers.

## Paragraph G

Richard Hession explains that people prefer to speak rather than listen.

Good salespeople take advantage of this by encouraging shoppers to talk about their needs.

This makes customers feel they are having a **consultation** rather than receiving a sales pitch.

Dr. Rob Yeung notes that salespeople build **rapport** by asking questions about family, lifestyle and hobbies.

This helps them appear likeable while gathering information about customer needs.

### Analysis

错误原因是把 **advertising** 和 **salespeople** 混在一起。

- information-oriented
- advertising

Paragraph D 讨论的是：

- logical reasoning
- informative appeals
- advertising strategies

- salespeople techniques
- consultation
- rapport

因此正确答案是 **D**。
